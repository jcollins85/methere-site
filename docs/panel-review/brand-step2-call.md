## 2. One-sentence reasoning

B preserves the verb-led parallelism with steps 1 and 3, drops both jargon traps ("venue" / "auto-fills"), and lets the scene fragment carry the recognition work without narrating UI in future tense (A's "You'll see") or restating the mechanic in instructional voice (C's "You tap the right one").

### Quick triage of the rejected options

- **A** — three sentences, ~24 words. "You'll see the places nearby" narrates UI in future tense, which the brand doesn't do (voice §3.9 — we direct, we don't tour-guide). Drops the unbothered register a hair.
- **C** — Scene-first construction matches brand-direction §2.2 ("product-copy-as-headline / short scenes the visitor recognizes themselves in") and is genuinely tempting. Killed by the closing "You tap the right one" — reads as instructional in a section that's already established this is an obvious mechanic. Also breaks the verb-led cadence of steps 1 and 3, which costs more than it gains given step 1 already does scene-fragmentation in its own body ("A nickname works. A description works.").

## 3. Other off-voice notes from steps 1 and 3 while I was there

Both pass the brand floor. Two minor flags worth surfacing for whoever holds the section copy:

1. **Step 3 body has redundancy with its own H2.** H2 says "Tap the alert." Body opens with the same beat ("Walk past again and your phone reminds you. Tap the notification to open MetHere..."). *Alert* and *notification* are interchangeable here — the body is restating the headline before adding new information. The sequence "open MetHere and see who you've saved at this place" is also slightly walkthrough-y for the brand register. Not a blocker, but if copywriter is touching the section anyway, this body could lose ~10 words and tighten. Suggested reframe direction (not a final line, copywriter writes it): drop the "tap the notification" restatement, go straight to what they see — *"Walk past again, your phone reminds you. Open it and there they are — everyone you've met here. That's the whole thing."* Final line is copywriter's call.

2. **Step 1 is clean.** The "A nickname works. A description works." pair is the most on-brand line in the section — keep it as-is.

3. **The visual frame is right.** Mint discipline holds (single mint accent on STEP 2 label + the in-app mint chip), dark surfaces, no gradient drift, screenshot framed cleanly without halos or perspective tilt. No brand-floor violations on the layout side.
